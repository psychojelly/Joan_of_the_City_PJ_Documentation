Bridging Dev & Show Design

Engineers and show designers describe the same work in different words. This page is the translation layer — so a "cue," a "scene," or "done" means one thing to everyone on Joan of the City.

Bridge

Why a shared language

An AR opera lives where a Unity project meets a theatre production. The same object is a "prefab" to one person and a "prop" to another; "it's done" can mean compiled or it can mean rehearsed and locked.

Most friction on a project like this isn't technical or artistic — it's vocabulary. When the two sides use each other's words on purpose, hand-offs get faster and fewer things get lost between the design pass and the stage.

🤝

Use this page in stand-ups and reviews. When someone says a loaded word — scene, cue, asset, done — point at the row and say which meaning you mean.

Overloaded words

A few words mean different things to each side. These cause the most confusion — say which you mean.

⚠️

"It works on my machine" is not "it works in the show." A cue is only done when it fires consistently across every headset, on-device — not just in the Editor.

Hand-offs & sign-off

The two points where work crosses the line between disciplines — and who has to say yes.

Design → Dev

A scene's visual language, AR interaction, asset requirements, cue structure, and measurements are handed to implementation. The clearer the cue sheet, the cleaner the build.

Dev → Design (rehearsal)

The built scene comes back for tech/dress rehearsal. Design gives notes against the real room, light, and timing; dev folds them in.

Dual sign-off

Anything touching a live scene gets one engineering review and one design review before it's locked (see Contributing).

Bridge

Common miscommunications

The recurring ones, and how to avoid them.

  • "Scene" ambiguity — clarify Unity scene vs show scene whenever it could go either way.
  • "Done" mismatch — agree that done = locked: built, on-device, and rehearsed.
  • Editor ≠ headset — looks and performance must be judged on-device, in the venue's light.
  • Silent cross-device drift — a cue that works on one headset can differ on another; verify across all.
  • Anchored vs follow — if content "moves with my head," it's Follow; the target is Anchor.
