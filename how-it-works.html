How It Works & Recommendations

The whole system in plain English — then practical guidance for running it reliably on Wi-Fi: optimizations and monitoring tools. For the deeper detail, see Brain PC & Glasses and System Overview.

In plain English

The whole system, simply

Think of it like a stage manager calling cues to a row of tiny self-contained theaters. Nothing is streamed — each headset already has the whole show and just reacts to calls.

  • Each headset runs the show itself. Every pair of XREAL glasses is driven by its own little computer (an Android device) that already has the entire show installed on it.
  • The “brain” is the control desk. A laptop runs a web page of buttons. Tapping a button shouts a numbered cue across the Wi-Fi — to every headset at once.
  • Cues just flip things on and off. When a headset hears a cue, it toggles that piece of the show — the video, the droplets, Joan's crown, the communion bodies.
  • Music is handled on each headset. Each headset downloads the audio tracks ahead of time (from the internet) and plays them locally, reacting to the same cues.
  • The glasses just display and track. They show what their computer draws and report where the wearer is looking. They don't compute the show.
  • Nothing reports back. The brain only shouts cues out — it never hears anything back from the headsets.
🎭

The mental model: the brain calls the cues; each headset performs the whole show on its own. Keeping them in step is purely a matter of every headset hearing every cue and running the same build.

Recommendations

Running it on Wi-Fi

Because cues fly over Wi-Fi as fire-and-forget messages (no delivery guarantee, no acknowledgement), network quality directly determines whether every headset stays in step. Treat the network as show-critical gear.

📶

Dedicated network

Run a private router/AP just for the show — never venue or public Wi-Fi. Fewer devices, less congestion, lower jitter.

🔌

Wire what you can

Put the brain laptop on Ethernet to the access point; keep only the headsets on Wi-Fi.

📡

5/6 GHz, clean channel

Use 5 GHz or Wi-Fi 6, not 2.4 GHz. Scan for a quiet channel; avoid crowded venue spectrum.

🏷️

Fixed IPs

Give each headset a static IP / DHCP reservation so the controller's saved IP list keeps working.

Reduce the live network's job

  • Pre-cache all audio stems onto every headset before doors. Stems come from GitHub over the internet by default — pre-warming the cache (or shipping them bundled) means the live network only carries tiny cue packets, and removes the internet dependency mid-show.
  • Keep the network light — disable auto-updates and background traffic on the headsets and the brain; keep unrelated devices off the network.
  • Strong, close signal — APs near the audience area, minimal walls between AP and headsets. Packet loss = a missed cue.
⚠️

Don't “resend cues to be safe.” Visual cues are toggles — sending the same cue twice flips it back off. Reliability has to come from a clean network, not from re-sending. If you need a safety net, build an explicit re-sync cue rather than blindly repeating.

Performance & optimization

Stutter on this rig is almost always local to a headset (it renders on mobile-class Android hardware), and most often it's loading colliding with playing. The fix is to separate load from play.

Separate loading from playback

  • Pre-warm on load: pre-instantiate the FlipBook's 31 meshes, activate heavy VFX once to compile shaders, Prepare() video before Play(), and pre-cache audio stems. Do all loading during a standby phase, not on the cue.
  • Toggle visibility, not existence where activation causes init spikes — keep objects loaded and hide/show them (trade memory for smoothness).
  • Use Local / pre-cached audio for cue-critical stems so nothing decodes or downloads at the moment of play.

Hold the frame budget

  • Set and enforce particle & texture budgets per scene — VFX Graph + SDF particles are GPU-heavy on the headset.
  • Bake lighting; keep real-time lights to a minimum.
  • Target a stable 72–90 fps; if it dips, reduce particle counts, texture sizes, and overdraw before anything else.
  • Watch thermals — sustained load throttles mobile chips, which reads as gradual “chugging.” The XREAL notification listener surfaces temperature warnings.
  • Profile on-device — the Editor under-reports XR cost. And keep every headset on the same build version.

Monitoring & tools

Because nothing reports back to the brain, you have to watch the system from the outside. A few tools per layer:

LayerToolsWhat it tells you
Headset performanceUnity Profiler (remote), Unity Frame DebuggerCPU/GPU/memory spikes, draw calls — confirms load-on-play vs steady saturation.
Android runtimeadb logcat, Perfetto/systrace, Snapdragon Profiler or Arm Streamline (per chipset)Runtime errors (e.g. NullReference on an unmapped cue), GPU/thermal traces.
OSC / cuesAn OSC monitor (e.g. Protokol), WiresharkConfirms cue packets are actually sent and arriving; reveals UDP packet loss.
NetworkRouter/AP admin page, a Wi-Fi analyzer appPer-client signal strength, channel congestion, who's hogging the air.
Audio cacheThe project's AudioCacheStatusDisplayWhether each headset has its stems cached (i.e. pre-warmed) before the show.
Thermals / batteryXREAL notification listener; device battery/temperatureEarly warning of throttling or a headset about to drop.
🩺

The missing piece: there's no health/heartbeat coming back from the headsets, so the operator can't tell if one missed a cue. Two mitigations: keep a human spotter watching the headsets during a run, and consider adding a lightweight heartbeat (each headset periodically logs or pings a dashboard) so drift is visible rather than discovered by the audience.

✅

Minimum show-day kit: an OSC monitor open on the brain to confirm cues fire, the router admin page up to watch signal, and the audio cache confirmed warm on every headset before doors.
