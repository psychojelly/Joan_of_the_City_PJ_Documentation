Assets

The asset pipeline

Most character assets begin as T-poses and become custom silhouettes and skins — primarily for Shadow and Communion. Particle systems carry libretto text and data. Everything heavy is tracked with Git LFS so the repo stays usable.

Start from the T-pose

Custom silhouettes & skins are built from T-poses for the Shadow and Communion presences, and for the remaining Joans as they come online (owner: Jack).

Clean up & optimize textures

Texture cleanup and an optimization pass keep each scene inside its particle/texture budget — performance is a standing priority.

Build particle & FX assets

Particle systems carry libretto text / data (owner: KK). Light FX (lilac sky, streaks, chromatic aberration) and Communion ripples are authored here too.

Bring into Unity via LFS

Place under the right scene folder and commit on an LFS-tracked path. Confirm with git lfs status before pushing.

Naming conventions

Predictable names make the cue sheet, search, and reviews painless. Lowercase, hyphen-separated, scoped by scene.

# pattern:  <scene>_<category>_<name>[_<variant>]

shadow_silhouette_crawl.fbx
voice_raven_hero.fbx
voice_anim_raven-land.anim
light_particle_streak.prefab
communion_skin_01.png
shared_particle_libretto.prefab     # reused across scenes
TokenValues
scenelight, voice, shadow, communion, real, hustle, ground, or shared
categorysilhouette, skin, particle, raven, audio, tex, anim, mat
nameDescriptive, hyphen-separated (raven-land)
variantOptional (emissive, lod1, alt)
🔗

Asset names should match the Asset column in the cue sheet, so a cue and its asset resolve to each other with no guesswork.

Typography

Display — Cormorant Garamond. A high-contrast serif for titles, libretto, and any sacred text. It carries the operatic, historical weight.

Interface — Inter. Neutral and legible for the rare diegetic labels and all operator/console tooling.

Mono — JetBrains Mono. Code, cue ids, asset names, cue sheets.

Light & material principles

  • Light tells the story. A scene's emotion is set by its key light and color before any character appears.
  • Subtractive design. The real venue is busy — virtual content should be sparse, deliberate, and luminous against the dark.
  • Glow & audio-reactivity. Characters and particles emit soft light; emissive intensity follows the audio.
  • Readable on-site. Assets must hold up in the venue's real light — test at the actual performance time and place.
