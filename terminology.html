Opera Terminology

A plain-language guide to the vocabulary of opera — so everyone on the team, from engineers to designers, can read the score, the script, and the cue sheet with the same words. Where a term shapes our build, it's noted.

Terminology

Text & Score

The written words and music — the source material every scene is built from.

TermMeaning
LibrettoThe text of the opera — all the words that are sung (and any spoken lines). Italian for "little book." Written by the librettist.
ScoreThe complete written music: every vocal and instrumental part notated together. A vocal score reduces the orchestra to piano under the voices.
StemAn individual mixed audio track — one instrument, voice, or layer — kept separate so it can be triggered or balanced on its own.
Leitmotif / motifA short, recurring musical idea tied to a character, place, or theme — it returns (often transformed) whenever that thing is present.
UnderscoreMusic played underneath action or speech to carry mood, rather than as a number the audience focuses on.
Terminology

Solo Forms

The ways a single character sings — from speech-like delivery that moves the story to the full emotional set-piece.

TermMeaning
AriaA self-contained solo song for one voice — the emotional high point where a character pauses to express what they feel. The "big number."
Recitative recitativoSung speech that carries dialogue and plot. Rhythmically free, following the patterns of talking. Secco = sparse accompaniment; accompagnato = with orchestra.
AriosoA middle ground between recitative and aria — lyrical and melodic, but shorter and less formal than a full aria.
CavatinaA short, simple, lyrical aria — often a character's first entrance.
CabalettaThe faster, driving final section that caps a two-part aria.
🎙️

Rule of thumb: recitative moves the story, the aria stops to feel it. Knowing which is which helps pace cues and performer action.

Terminology

Ensemble & Chorus

When more than one voice sings at once.

TermMeaning
Duet / Trio / QuartetTwo, three, or four soloists singing together.
EnsembleSeveral soloists singing simultaneously — often each with their own words and feelings layered at once.
ChorusA large group of singers acting as a crowd or collective voice — and the music they sing.
FinaleThe extended closing number of an act, usually gathering soloists and chorus into a big build.
Terminology

Structure

How an opera is divided up, from the orchestral opening to the breaks between sections.

TermMeaning
OvertureThe orchestral piece that opens the opera, before any singing — often previewing themes to come.
PreludeA shorter orchestral introduction that frequently leads straight into the first scene.
ActA major division of the work. Operas are commonly built from two to five acts.
SceneA subdivision within an act. (In Joan of the City, "scene" is our core unit — Light, Voice, Shadow, Communion.)
Interlude / IntermezzoAn instrumental passage between scenes or acts, covering a transition.
IntermissionThe interval — the audience break between acts.
Terminology

Voices & the Pit

The vocal ranges and the people who run the music.

Voice types (high → low)

VoiceMeaning
SopranoThe highest female (or treble) voice — frequently the heroine.
Mezzo-sopranoThe middle female voice, warmer than a soprano.
Contralto (alto)The lowest female voice.
CountertenorThe highest male voice, singing in the alto/soprano range.
TenorThe highest common male voice — often the romantic lead.
BaritoneThe middle male voice.
BassThe lowest male voice — authority figures, elders, villains.

Related terms

TermMeaning
ColoraturaRapid, elaborate ornamentation — runs, trills, and leaps — often for a high, agile voice.
TessituraThe part of the range where a role mostly sits (distinct from its absolute highest/lowest notes).
RegisterA section of the vocal range with a consistent quality (e.g. chest vs head voice).
Conductor / MaestroLeads the musicians and singers, setting tempo and shaping the performance.
PitThe lowered area where the orchestra plays, typically in front of and below the stage.
SurtitlesTranslated or transcribed text displayed for the audience (above or beside the stage).

In Joan of the City

How these classical terms show up in an opera told through XR. Same vocabulary, new medium.

📜

Libretto → particles

The libretto text and data are integrated into particle systems — the words become part of the visuals.

🎚️

Score → stems & cue sheet

The score is delivered as stems, triggered scene by scene from the audio cue sheet.

🎭

Scene → our core unit

Where a classical opera nests scenes inside acts, we build directly in scenes: Light, Voice, Shadow, Communion.

🔁

Leitmotif → behavior

Recurring elements like the Hero Raven function as a visual leitmotif — a motif you see, not only hear.

🔗
