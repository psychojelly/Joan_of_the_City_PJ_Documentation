Design Style Guide

The shared visual and experiential language for Joan of the City. It keeps every scene, asset, and interface feeling like one world — operatic, restrained, and anchored in real space.

Style Guide

Design principles

Six commitments. When a decision is hard, return to these.

🎭

Operatic

High contrast, theatrical light, emotional scale. The medium is new; the feeling is opera.

➖

Subtractive

The real venue is busy. Virtual content is sparse, deliberate, and luminous against the dark.

📍

Anchored

Content belongs to a place in the real world — Anchor, not Follow. It never drifts with the head.

🎶

Audio-reactive

Light, particles, and Communion respond to the score. Sound and image move as one.

🕊️

Character, not effect

The Hero Raven, the Shadow, the Communion presence act with intention — never decoration.

🛟

Safe & legible

Comfortable to watch, readable on-site, and always able to STOP ALL to a safe state.

Style Guide

Typography

Three typefaces, three jobs. Used consistently across the docs, decks, and any in-experience text.

RoleTypefaceUsed for
DisplayCormorant GaramondTitles, libretto, any sacred / operatic text. Carries the historical weight.
InterfaceInterBody copy, labels, UI, console tooling. Neutral and legible.
MonoJetBrains MonoCode, cue ids, asset names, cue sheets.
🔤

Pair a large display serif with quiet sans-serif body. Don't set long passages in the display serif, and don't use mono for anything but data and code.

Style Guide

Motion & feel

How things move sells the world. Motion should feel composed, not switched on.

  • Gradual evolution. Builds are slow and continuous — sky grows, streaks accumulate, ripples spread. Avoid hard on/off pops.
  • Audio-reactive. Tie intensity, growth, and glow to the score so visuals breathe with the music.
  • Intentional entrances & exits. The Hero Raven separates, approaches, and stays; the collage tears off and flies away rather than fading.
  • Atmosphere over flash. Chromatic aberration, drift, and particle reveals add depth — used as seasoning, not spectacle.
Style Guide

Spatial & AR UX

Designing for a head-worn, in-the-room experience. Comfort and safety come before spectacle.

✅

Do: keep key action within a comfortable cone of vision; anchor content to stable, textured surfaces; lead attention with light and sound; design for the audience's real standing points; test in the venue's actual light.

🚫

Don't: head-lock UI or float menus during a scene; force rapid head-turns to follow a beat; lure anyone toward hazards; rely on a single sense for a critical cue; trust how it looks in the Editor over how it looks on-device.

Style Guide

Design review checklist

Run a scene or asset against this before it's called done.

  • Reads as one world — palette, type, and light match the rest of the show.
  • Content is anchored and holds its place across devices.
  • Motion builds gradually and reacts to audio.
  • Named elements (ravens, presences) behave with intention, not as effects.
  • Comfortable to watch; no head-locked UI during the scene.
  • Legible in the venue's real light at performance time.
  • STOP ALL returns the scene to a safe, neutral state.
